## opener

Sunday, July 19. Contrastive alignment spec unwritten — 13 weeks. GSPA unread — day 36. "When Attention Collapses" (STS) unread — day 25. Visual Access Boundaries unread — day 5.

Today's digest raises two high-value issues alongside the usual competitor pressure.

**Beyond Single Expert: Harmonizing Diverse Visual Priors in MLLMs** (Lin et al., arXiv 7/16) introduces ViPS, which shows empirically that diverse foundation model priors (depth, shape, semantic) are complementary in MLLMs for spatial understanding, and that single-prior designs are strictly suboptimal. This is a direct threat to surf's current prior module framing: surf uses line density as a single structural prior. If ViPS holds in manipulation, the prior module needs to harmonize at minimum line density + depth (DRACO, DepthCache), and possibly V4-dispersity. The alternative reading — that complementarity between priors validates that each prior is independently useful — is also consistent with the findings, but it means surf's single-prior result is only a lower bound. Read ViPS before finalizing the prior module design in the spec.

**Information-dependent eye-hand coordination emerges from active vision** (Zhao et al., S2 2026-06-02) gives the computational principle surf's [[eval/gaze]] has been missing: in continuous motor tasks (driving, reading), saccade-pursuit patterns emerge because gaze allocates to regions that maximally reduce task-relevant uncertainty. The underlying principle — gaze goes where information is most decision-relevant — is exactly the criterion surf's contrastive scorer should implement. Two direct applications: (1) the uncertainty-reduction stopping criterion in this paper maps directly onto [[claims/iterative selection]] — stop refining token selection when marginal task-relevant uncertainty reduction drops below the budget cost; (2) [[eval/gaze]] should be measuring whether surf-selected tokens co-locate with the regions that maximize action-uncertainty reduction, not just human fixation density. This is the paper that formally grounds what "task-relevant gaze" means for manipulation. Read before writing [[eval/gaze]] design into the spec.

**RoboTTT** (Jiang et al., S2 7/16) scales robot policy context to 8K timesteps without growing inference latency via Test-Time-Training. This is a paradigm-level competitor to surf's iterative selection framing: instead of selecting which tokens are informative at each step, RoboTTT feeds all tokens from all historical steps. The architectural choice surf faces is sparse selection + structural prior (surf) vs. dense history + TTT compression (RoboTTT). Both spend compute to extract historical information; the question is whether structural inductive bias (line density) is necessary when 8K-step context is available. This is an empirical question on LIBERO.

Write the spec.

## questions

- Beyond Single Expert × surf prior module: ViPS shows diverse structural priors are complementary for spatial understanding in MLLMs. In surf's manipulation context, does depth (DRACO) + line drawings (surf) select different or overlapping contact-region patches on LIBERO? If complementary, the prior module needs a harmonization mechanism rather than a single prior. If overlapping, line density is the sufficient single signal and depth redundantly confirms it.
- Information-dependent eye-hand × [[eval/gaze]]: The computational model grounds gaze as uncertainty-reduction-driven. Does the same principle hold in VLA manipulation? If the VLA policy's attention maps are calibrated to task-relevant uncertainty, they should co-locate with surf's line-density selection on high-uncertainty contact regions. Testable by computing policy attention entropy over surf-selected vs. surf-excluded patches.
- Information-dependent eye-hand × [[claims/iterative selection]]: The paper's stopping criterion (stop when marginal uncertainty reduction drops below budget cost) is the same criterion Kumar et al. (probing-based SHT, 7/18) gives for token-inspection stopping. Are these the same formalism at different levels of abstraction, or do they make distinct predictions for surf's budget policy?
- RoboTTT × [[claims/iterative selection]]: RoboTTT compresses 8K timesteps to constant latency. At what context length does dense-history TTT outperform surf's per-step structural selection on LIBERO? This determines whether surf's inductive bias is useful in the low-data/short-context regime or is universally dominated by a large enough context.
- Reflex × surf efficiency framing: If flow-matching VLA denoising loops dominate latency (and Reflex solves that), how much of the remaining latency is visual encoding? Is token reduction at the visual input stage the right efficiency lever, or should surf's contribution be framed as selection quality (better tokens, not fewer tokens)?
- Active evaluation × surf experiments: The factor-based active evaluation approach identifies which object-pose × viewpoint × instruction combinations to test for statistical robustness. On LIBERO, which factor combinations are hardest for surf's structural prior to generalize over? Camera-frame misalignment (raised by "See like a Robot," day 6) is one such factor. What is the minimum factor set for a rigorous contrastive alignment evaluation?
- Divergent gaze × [[eval/gaze]] population anchor: If trained artists represent deliberate, structure-driven gaze allocation, are their fixation patterns closer to action-relevant attention than neurotypical observers? And is there a robot-domain analog — human demonstrators who explicitly plan contact regions while watching a manipulation scene — that could serve as the right gaze supervisor for surf?
