# 2026-07-04

## opener

Saturday, July 4. GSPA unread — day 21. "When Attention Collapses" (STS) unread — day 10. Contrastive alignment spec unwritten — 7+ weeks.

Today's lead: Bridge-WA (Bai et al., arXiv 2607.02195) proposes a different question than surf's current contrastive scorer: instead of "which patches align with the instruction embedding," it asks "where and how will the world change as a result of this action?" It builds compact world-change predictors over spatially-indexed features, explicitly avoiding dense generation in favor of learning which scene regions are control-relevant. This reframes the "task-relevant patch" problem: the right selection criterion may not be language alignment but predicted visual change under the policy's actions. The two framings converge when the instruction fully specifies the action, but diverge when the same instruction can be executed in multiple ways — in that case, action-relevance tracks pose and trajectory, not semantics. Surf needs to be explicit about which regime it targets.

Second: Zhao et al. (bioRxiv 2026-06-02) formalize eye-hand coordination as dual MPC — eye movements minimize task-relevant uncertainty, and the resulting internal model drives hand trajectory planning. This is a principled formalization of the active-vision problem surf sits in. If surf's patch selector is the discrete analogue of their eye-movement policy, the training signal should be reduction of uncertainty about the action (analogous to their MPC criterion), not just contrastive alignment with the instruction embedding. The two criteria may agree on manipulation scenes where the instruction specifies the contact region, but the MPC formulation is arguably more general and testable.

## questions

- Bridge-WA action-relevance × surf language-alignment: on manipulation tasks where the instruction fully specifies the contact region ("pick up the red cup"), do the patches that predict action-induced scene changes co-locate with the patches that have the highest instruction-embedding score? If yes, the two criteria agree and either works. If no, which criterion selects more task-relevant patches? This is testable on LIBERO or BridgeV2 by running both selectors and comparing which patch mask leads to better action prediction.
- MPC active-vision criterion → surf training objective: if surf's selector is the discrete analogue of Zhao et al.'s eye-movement policy, can "minimize task-relevant uncertainty given selected patches" replace the contrastive language-alignment objective in [[experiments/contrastive alignment]]? What is the operational definition of "task-relevant uncertainty" at the patch level — prediction error on the action token, or entropy of the action distribution conditioned on selected patches?
- Finetuning layers × token reduction placement: if only a small fraction of VLA layers drive action generation (Nguyen et al.), should surf apply aggressive token reduction at the non-critical early layers and normal token count at the critical layers? What's the inference cost of layer-selective token budgeting vs. uniform reduction across all layers?
- Declarative/procedural split × surf scorer training: should surf's contrastive patch scorer be trained in the declarative pretraining phase (language-visual alignment) before joint VLA finetuning? The Tsagkas et al. result suggests that mixing the phases causes procedural learning to overwrite declarative grounding — if surf's structural prior is part of declarative grounding, it should be frozen or preloaded before action finetuning begins.
- BrainDiVE × V4 prior operationalization: can the BrainDiVE optimization framework (diffusion + gradient ascent on target brain region activation) produce a per-category spatial filter that acts as surf's V4 prior? What is the resolution of the resulting spatial prior, and does it produce patch-level selection maps or only image-level preferred stimuli?
